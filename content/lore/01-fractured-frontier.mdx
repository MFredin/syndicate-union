Long before the Union Charter was drafted, the outer reach was a patchwork of convenience. Small crews banded together for a single campaign, split the salvage, and drifted apart before the season ended. It worked, in the way that anything works when the alternative is doing it alone — but it left the frontier's newcomers with nowhere steady to land.

Commanders who had spent three seasons building a syndicate from nothing watched it dissolve in a week when the founder logged off for good. Guides were scattered across a dozen private notes, half of them outdated, none of them maintained. Diplomacy meant whoever happened to be online when a rival syndicate wanted to talk.

It wasn't a crisis, exactly. It was just how things were — until a handful of commanders started asking whether it had to stay that way.
