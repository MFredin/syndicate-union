Syndicate Union's first real test came during a defensive operation against a much larger rival syndicate contesting one of the Union's early outposts. Squads showed up, but coordination was informal — voice calls with no clear chain of command, formations improvised mid-fight. The outpost held, barely, and only because the rival syndicate disengaged first.

It was close enough to a disaster that nobody wanted to repeat it. In the weeks after, a small group of pilots who'd led squads during the fight began documenting what had actually happened: which formations held, which calls came too late, what a real chain of command would have looked like.

That documentation became the seed of Fleet Command's doctrine. What started as a lessons-learned document turned into standing formations, defined squadron roles, and a rotation system for keeping fresh pilots in extended fights — the same fundamentals Fleet Command still teaches new pilots today.
